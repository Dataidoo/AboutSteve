// THE OLD WEB

You're driving.

You open the app on purpose. You type the search yourself. You close the tab when you're done. The tool waits for you. The screen serves the question you brought to it. This is what the web was supposed to be — a place you visit, on terms you set.

// THE NEW WEB

It's driving you.

The feed knows your mood before you do. The notification arrives at the precise moment you'd be most likely to tap. Forty minutes vanish. You came for one thing and left with a profile that's a little more complete. You didn't choose the path. It chose you.

// THE QUESTION NOBODY ASKS

Every morning the feed knows your mood before you do. Every search bar finishes your thought before you've thought it.

The interface bends — gently, helpfully — until the shape of your day fits the shape of someone else's metric.

You opened the app to check one thing. Forty minutes later you're three accounts deep on a stranger's vacation, served by a system that learned exactly which stranger would keep you there.

That wasn't a choice. That was a pull.

// HOW IT WORKS

A web you actually steer.

You sign in once. Your data stays on your device, encrypted, portable. The apps you use request access — explicitly, per-session — and the answer is always yours to give or revoke.

No silent profiles. No predictive nudges baked into the UI. Search returns what you asked for, in the order it was found, not the order someone paid for.

// WHERE IT FAILS

Honest about the trade-offs.

It's slower. There's no algorithm pre-loading your next dopamine hit, so the first week feels quiet — almost broken. Discovery takes effort again.

Network effects work against us: friends still live on the old platforms, so for a while you'll be in two places at once. We're not pretending that's nothing.
